Conclusion

Our study focused on understanding heat-stress vulnerability in Yangon. Through our methods, we identified that:

  1. Rural townships are most vulnerable to heat-stress, due to less favourable social variables.
  2. Townships will experience differing degrees of heat exposure and hence differing heat vulnerability across months / seasons.
  3. Therefore, it can be more productive for authorities to look into addressing social vulnerability, as exposure variables (i.e. LST) can be difficult to alter.

It was useful to adopt remote sensing methods and GIS analysis as we can spatially determine areas / townships which experience high temperatures and also account for the social variables which contribute to overall heat-stress vulnerability in Yangon in a cross-data comparison and analysis. Future studies can look at incorporating more social and physical variables in identifying heat-stress vulnerability in Yangon, such as using a wider variety of climate data (ambient, relative humidity etc.) to better determine the perceived heat and outdoor thermal comfort of the population, and more accurately determine heat-stress vulnerability. As our study serves as a starting point to understand heat-stress vulnerability in Yangon, we focused on the township level of analysis. Therefore, further research and delve into breaking down townships (especially large ones), into climate zones, to better capture the distribution of LST across Yangon. This vulnerability analysis was not compared or validated with actual health data (i.e. statistics on visits to clinics for a heat-related health issue), hence future studies may look to incorporate them as part of their research method.
